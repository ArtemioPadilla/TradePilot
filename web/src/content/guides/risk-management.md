# Risk Management for Traders

Effective risk management is the difference between long-term survival and catastrophic loss. No trading strategy, however profitable in backtests, can succeed without a disciplined approach to controlling downside risk.

## Position Sizing

Position sizing determines how much capital to allocate to each trade. The goal is to ensure that no single loss can significantly damage your portfolio.

**Percentage-of-equity method**: Risk a fixed percentage of your total portfolio on each trade, typically between 1% and 3%. If your portfolio is worth $100,000 and you risk 2% per trade, your maximum loss per position is $2,000. This approach automatically scales position sizes up as your portfolio grows and down during drawdowns.

**Kelly Criterion**: A mathematical formula that calculates the optimal fraction of capital to wager based on your strategy's win rate and payoff ratio. While theoretically optimal, full Kelly sizing produces extreme volatility. Most practitioners use half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly to smooth the equity curve.

The key principle is consistency. Choose a sizing method and apply it uniformly rather than increasing size after wins or decreasing after losses based on emotion.

## Stop Losses

A stop loss is a predetermined exit point that limits the downside on any individual trade. Without stop losses, small losses can compound into portfolio-ending drawdowns.

**Fixed stop**: Set a hard price level below your entry where you will exit regardless of other signals. For example, placing a stop 5% below your purchase price caps the loss on that position.

**Trailing stop**: A dynamic stop that moves upward as the price rises but never moves down. This locks in gains while still providing downside protection. A 10% trailing stop on a stock that rises from $100 to $150 would trigger a sell at $135.

**Volatility-based stop**: Set stops based on the asset's Average True Range (ATR) or standard deviation. This adapts to each asset's behavior rather than applying a one-size-fits-all threshold.

The critical discipline is honoring your stops. Moving a stop loss further away to avoid being stopped out defeats the entire purpose of risk management.

## Diversification

Diversification reduces portfolio risk by spreading capital across assets that do not move in lockstep.

**Across asset classes**: Combine equities, bonds, commodities, and alternative investments. These asset classes often respond differently to economic conditions, reducing overall portfolio volatility.

**Within asset classes**: Hold positions across multiple sectors, geographies, and market capitalizations. Concentrated bets on a single sector expose you to industry-specific shocks.

**Across strategies**: Run multiple uncorrelated strategies simultaneously. A momentum strategy may struggle in choppy markets where a mean reversion strategy thrives, and vice versa.

True diversification requires low correlation between holdings, not simply owning many assets. Holding ten technology stocks is concentration, not diversification. Measure the correlation matrix of your holdings periodically and rebalance when correlations drift upward.

## Putting It Together

Risk management is not a single technique but a layered system: position sizing limits per-trade risk, stop losses enforce those limits mechanically, and diversification ensures that correlated losses across positions do not compound into catastrophic drawdowns.
