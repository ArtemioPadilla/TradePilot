# Trading Strategy Types

Systematic trading strategies replace discretionary decision-making with repeatable, rules-based approaches. Understanding the major strategy families helps you choose the right approach for your market view, risk tolerance, and time horizon.

## Momentum Strategies

Momentum investing is built on the observation that assets which have performed well recently tend to continue performing well, and those that have performed poorly tend to keep declining. This phenomenon has been documented across equities, commodities, currencies, and bonds.

**How it works**: Rank assets by their returns over a lookback period, typically 3 to 12 months. Buy the top performers and sell or avoid the bottom performers. Rebalance periodically to capture shifting trends.

**Why it works**: Behavioral finance suggests that investors underreact to new information, causing trends to persist. Institutional flows and herding behavior reinforce the effect as rising prices attract more buyers.

**Risks**: Momentum strategies are vulnerable to sharp reversals, often called momentum crashes. These tend to occur during market regime changes, such as the recovery from a bear market, when previous losers suddenly outperform. Position sizing and stop losses are essential to surviving these drawdowns.

## Mean Reversion Strategies

Mean reversion is the counterpart to momentum. It assumes that prices eventually return to a long-term average, so assets that have moved significantly away from that average are likely to reverse.

**How it works**: Identify assets trading below their historical average, such as a stock whose price is well below its 200-day moving average. Buy the undervalued assets and sell the overvalued ones, expecting a reversion to the mean.

**Common signals**: Bollinger Band breakouts, RSI extremes (below 30 for oversold, above 70 for overbought), and z-score deviations from moving averages. Pairs trading is a related approach that identifies two historically correlated assets and trades the spread when it diverges.

**Risks**: The core danger is that "cheap" assets can keep getting cheaper. A stock trading below its average may be declining for fundamental reasons such as deteriorating earnings, regulatory action, or structural disruption. Mean reversion strategies require strict entry and exit rules to avoid holding positions that never revert.

## Factor Investing

Factor investing systematically targets specific drivers of return that have been identified through decades of academic research. Rather than picking individual stocks, factor strategies tilt portfolios toward characteristics associated with long-term outperformance.

**Common factors**:
- **Value**: Buy assets that are cheap relative to fundamentals such as book value, earnings, or cash flow.
- **Size**: Small-cap stocks have historically outperformed large caps over long periods.
- **Quality**: Companies with strong balance sheets, high profitability, and stable earnings tend to outperform.
- **Low volatility**: Less volatile stocks have delivered higher risk-adjusted returns than theory predicts.
- **Momentum**: As described above, recent winners tend to keep winning.

**Multi-factor approaches**: Combining several factors in a single portfolio can improve diversification and smooth returns, since different factors outperform in different market environments. For example, value and momentum tend to be negatively correlated, so holding both reduces drawdown severity.

**Risks**: Individual factors can underperform for extended periods. Value investing, for instance, lagged growth strategies for much of the 2010s. Factor crowding occurs when too many investors pursue the same factor, compressing the premium and increasing the risk of sudden reversals.

## Choosing a Strategy

No single strategy works in all market conditions. Momentum thrives in trending markets, mean reversion in range-bound ones, and factor investing across full market cycles. The most robust approach is often a blend of strategies with low correlation to each other, combined with disciplined risk management.
